Malaria In The World

May 2016
Skills used: D3, HTML, CSS, JavaScript, JSON, Photoshop, Illustrator

View live visualization
(Works best on large screens with Firefox, Safari, or Chrome)


Overview

Mosquitoes are the most deadly animals in the world, killing more people than even humans per year. One of the most dangerous mosquito-borne diseases is malaria, which is caused by parasites that are transferred by the insect. The disease has become a widespread crisis, especially in Africa. In this data visualization, I wanted to show where on Earth malaria exists and how its prevalence has changed in the past decade and a half. I also wanted to make connections with climate data to draw conclusions about the environmental influences on the disease.



Process

The first step in the process was to acquire and parse through all the data available. I knew I wanted to concentrate on malaria, but I wanted other variables to help paint a bigger picture. I knew that malaria was most prevalent in Africa, but I wanted to know why. Was it because of heat, humidity, precipitation, distance from the ocean, or some other environmental factors? I didn't think I would be able to crack any mysteries, but I thought it would be at least interesting to try to see some correlations. I ended up gathering yearly malaria, population, temperature, and precipitation statistics from The World Bank and the World Health Organization.

The next step was to figure out the best way to display so much data at the same time. I came up with a few ideas and got some peer feedback. I found that it would be best to show geographically the location of malaria, alongside climate data in a scatter plot form. That way, users could see the connection between geography and climate by rolling over each country on the map or its complementary circle on the scatter plot. I used color in both graphs to show the amount of malaria deaths for the current year shown. Users would be able to drag the slider and see the data change for each year, showing how the disease has spread geographically in this last decade and a half. Mapping temperature on the x-axis and then precipitation on the y-axis then was able to show correlations between which countries have malaria and their climates.

Takeaways

The hardest part of this project was deciding how I wanted to present the data visually. I discovered the great value of getting a second or third opinion. Having another set of eyes can be a good test of whether the user would be able to understand the information, because often I am too involved in the project to have that perspective. In data visualization, organization is fundamental in how viewers will be able to see the data and understand.
